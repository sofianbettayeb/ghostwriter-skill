# Skill: /category — Name & Claim Your Category

## What this does

Helps you define, name, and own a specific content niche.
Not positioning in the marketing sense. The writing category you want to be known for.
The goal: when someone lands anywhere in your topic area, they find you.

---

## When to use

Run `/category` when:
- You're not sure what you write about (or it's too broad)
- You write about multiple topics and want to find the thread
- You want to stop competing and start owning a space
- You're building a content library and need a north star
- Your bio, content, and audience don't feel aligned

---

## The category framework

### What a category is NOT
- A job title ("I'm a marketer")
- A broad topic ("I write about SEO")
- A credential ("I have 10 years of experience")

### What a category IS
A specific intersection of:
- **Who you help** (the audience)
- **What problem you solve** (the outcome)
- **How you solve it** (your method or point of view)

Examples:
- "I help Webflow agencies win more clients through AEO methodology"
- "I teach first-time founders how to write content that builds trust before the sales call"
- "I cover the practical side of AI tools for solo marketers — no hype, no vague use cases"

### The Naming step
A named category is more powerful than a described one.
It gives people a label to attach to you.

Naming approaches:
- Coin a term: "AEO Copilot", "Digital Writer", "Category Design"
- Own an intersection: "Webflow SEO", "trust-first marketing"
- Flip the conventional wisdom: "anti-hustle marketing", "SEO without tricks"

### The Claiming step
Claiming means publishing consistently in your category until you own it.
Not in volume. In specificity.

Three ways to claim:
1. Every piece of content points back to your category
2. Your bio names the category explicitly
3. You have a flagship piece (article, guide, tool) that defines the category

---

## Using data to refine your category

The category you start with is rarely the final one.
Publish, observe, adjust.

Questions to ask after 30 days of publishing:
- Which posts got the most engagement? What do they have in common?
- Who is engaging? Is that who you wanted?
- What topic kept coming up that you didn't expect?
- Where did readers push back? (Pushback = you're saying something real)

---

## Questions to ask before writing

If the user hasn't provided enough context, ask:
1. What do you currently write about?
2. Who do you most want to be known by? (audience)
3. What's the one problem you want to be the go-to person for?
4. Is there a term or phrase you already use to describe what you do?

```
─────────────────────────────────────
CURRENT CATEGORY (as best as I can tell):
[One sentence — what the user currently writes about]

PROBLEM WITH CURRENT CATEGORY:
[Too broad / too generic / no clear audience / no POV — one sentence]

─────────────────────────────────────
PROPOSED CATEGORY OPTIONS:

Option A — Intersection:
[Who + problem + method]
Name candidate: [a short label for this category]

Option B — Contrarian angle:
[Flip the conventional wisdom in your space]
Name candidate: [a short label]

Option C — Niche down:
[Narrower version of what you already do]
Name candidate: [a short label]

─────────────────────────────────────
RECOMMENDED: Option [X]
WHY: [One sentence — why this is most defensible and ownable]

CLAIM IT BY:
1. [Bio update suggestion]
2. [Flagship content suggestion]
3. [First 3 posts to write to establish the category]
```
